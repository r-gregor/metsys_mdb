High CPU usage and "shaking" or stuttering video on antiX occur because low-end hardware often
lacks the power to decode modern YouTube video formats (VP9 or AV1) in software:

1. Force a Lighter Video Format (Highest Impact)
Modern YouTube videos are heavy. You can force YouTube to use an older, lighter format (H.264) that your
computer's hardware can likely handle more easily.  Install the "h264ify" or "enhanced-h264ify" extension in
your browser (Firefox or Chrome/Chromium).  In the extension settings, check the boxes to block VP8, VP9, and
AV1. This offloads work from your CPU to your graphics chip (GPU).

2. Enable Hardware Acceleration
Browsers on Linux often have hardware acceleration disabled by default, which maxes out the CPU.  In Firefox:
Go to about:config, search for gfx.webrender.all, and set it to true.  In Chromium/Chrome: Go to Settings >
System and ensure "Use graphics acceleration when available" is toggled ON.

3. Use a Lightweight Video Player (Alternative to Browsers)
Browsers are resource-heavy. You can watch YouTube videos directly through a lightweight media player.
SMPlayer with YouTube Support: antiX often comes with SMPlayer. Open it, go to Options > YouTube Browser (or
press F11), and search for videos there. It uses much less CPU than a web browser.  MPV Player: You can play a
video directly by typing mpv [URL] in the terminal.

4. Adjust System & Video Settings
Lower Resolution: If 1080p is shaking, switch the YouTube gear icon setting to 720p or 480p.  Check
Compositor: In antiX, if you have a compositor like Compton or Picom running, it might cause screen tearing or
"shaking." Try disabling it temporarily through the antiX Control Center to see if playback improves.  Check
Adblockers: Recent YouTube updates have caused high CPU usage when certain adblockers are active. Try
disabling your adblocker or switching to uBlock Origin, which is generally better for performance.
